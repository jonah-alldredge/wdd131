GIANT

Giant was founded in 1972 in Dajia, Taiwan, by King Liu. Giant began as an OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer). Their big break came in 1977 when they struck a massive deal to manufacture bikes for Schwinn, which was the dominant American brand at the time.

The turning point for the brand happened in the mid-80s. After a labor strike at a Schwinn plant, Schwinn began looking for other partners, and Giant realized they were too dependent on a single client. Giant began selling bikes under its own "Giant" brand name. As the partnership with Schwinn faded, Giant launched globally, starting in Europe and moving into the US.

TREK

The company began in a small, rented red barn in Waterloo, Wisconsin, with a team of just five employees. In their first year, they hand-built nearly 900 steel touring frames. By 1979, sales had already reached nearly $2 million, proving there was a massive appetite for premium domestic bikes.

Trek quickly became a pioneer in frame materials and technology. In the 1990s and 2000s, Trek became a household name through professional cycling.

CANNONDALE

Cannondale didn’t start in a corporate boardroom—it started in a workshop over a pickle factory in Connecticut. Born in 1971, the brand was built on a simple, rebellious premise: there had to be a better way. While the rest of the cycling world was obsessed with traditional steel frames and rigid rules, Cannondale set out to "ditch the rulebook."

Cannondale’s journey is fueled by a mix of digital invention and raw creativity. Often called "crazy" for their designs, they’ve spent over 50 years proving their critics wrong by winning championships and reinventing how people experience the ride.
